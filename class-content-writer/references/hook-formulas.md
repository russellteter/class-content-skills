# Opening Hook Formulas — From Published Class Content

## Hook 1: The Relatable Struggle
Put the reader in a specific, visceral scenario they've experienced.

**Published example:**
> Many trainers and instructional designers have high hopes when launching breakout rooms in the virtual classroom. They expect groups to dive into the task, collaborate, and return with new ideas, ready to present — but instead encounter disengagement and confusion.

**Another:**
> Admitting 40 participants, fielding audio issues, answering where course materials are, managing screen shares while juggling slides, notes, and participant video feeds — within 90 seconds, instructors juggle windows, tabs, chats, and decisions.

**Template:**
> [Role] expects [positive outcome] when they [action]. Instead, they get [specific frustration]. The gap between what [training/VILT] should be and what it actually feels like is where most organizations lose [engagement/time/outcomes].

**When to use:** How-to posts, operational topics, instructor-focused content.

**Rules:**
- Name the role (trainer, L&D director, facilitator)
- Name the specific scenario (launching breakout rooms, managing 40 participants)
- Name the specific frustration (disengagement, audio issues, tool-switching)
- 2-3 sentences max

## Hook 2: The Data Point
Lead with a third-party statistic that establishes scale or urgency.

**Published example:**
> According to LinkedIn's 2025 Workplace Learning Report, "91% of L&D professionals believe continuous learning is more critical than ever for career success," while 88% of companies cite retention as a top concern.

**Another:**
> According to Class's survey with Training magazine and Microsoft of over 650 L&D professionals, "94 percent of respondents utilize VILT as part of their training process."

**Template:**
> According to [Source Name]'s [Report/Survey Name], [specific statistic with percentage]. [Second data point that creates tension or reinforces the first]. For organizations running [relevant context], this means [implication].

**When to use:** Trend analysis, thought leadership, ROI-focused content.

**Rules:**
- Name the source (not "a recent study")
- Use a specific percentage or number
- Create tension: the data point should reveal a gap, trend, or counterintuitive finding
- 2-3 sentences

## Hook 3: The Business Challenge
Frame a universal organizational challenge with specific context.

**Published example:**
> Organizations frequently attempt to repurpose general video conferencing tools for training purposes, which proves inadequate.

**Another:**
> Most virtual training programs prioritize the event itself. Once learners complete coursework, momentum halts.

**Template:**
> [Specific type of organization] is [trying to do X] with [current approach]. The result: [specific negative outcome]. The gap isn't in [what they know/their content/their instructors] — it's in [what their tools let them do/see/measure].

**When to use:** Product-positioning content, platform comparison topics, category-level arguments.

**Rules:**
- Be specific about the type of organization
- Name what they're using now (meeting tools, generic platforms)
- Name the specific gap
- Don't blame the people — blame the tools

## Hook 4: The Customer Scenario
Open with a specific customer's situation.

**Published example:**
> Volvo manages two distinct learning groups across 90+ countries with over 100,000 employees, and needed to adapt post-COVID.

**Template:**
> [Company name] [does X] across [scale metric]. When [change/challenge hit], their training team faced [specific problem]. [What they needed/what was at stake].

**When to use:** Customer stories, case study posts.

**Rules:**
- Name the company
- Include scale (countries, employees, sessions)
- Name the specific challenge
- 2-3 sentences

## Hook 5: The Counterintuitive Finding
Lead with a research finding that challenges assumptions.

**Published example:**
> Research revealed that 73% of respondents use Microsoft Teams as their primary virtual training platform, with 52% using Zoom. While popular for general communication, these tools weren't developed with instructional design principles in mind.

**Template:**
> [Research/survey] found that [surprising statistic]. Despite [widespread behavior], [counterintuitive gap or problem]. The issue isn't [what people assume] — it's [what the data reveals].

**When to use:** Research-backed posts, thought leadership, category positioning.

**Rules:**
- The finding should create productive tension
- Don't make the reader feel stupid for their current approach
- Bridge from the surprising data to a constructive framing that shows a path forward
- Never start with "Did you know" — lead with the source and the finding
- 2-3 sentences

## Choosing the Right Hook

| Hook Type | Best For | Energy Level |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Relatable Struggle | How-to posts, instructor topics | Medium — empathetic |
| Data Point | Trend analysis, thought leadership | High — authoritative |
| Business Challenge | Platform positioning, category arguments | Medium — analytical |
| Customer Scenario | Case studies, customer stories | Low-medium — narrative |
| Counterintuitive Finding | Research posts, category positioning | High — provocative |

**General hook rules:**
1. 2-3 sentences maximum before the reader knows what the post is about.
2. Specific beats general. Named roles, real numbers, concrete scenarios.
3. No throat-clearing. If the first sentence could be deleted without losing meaning, delete it.
4. Match the hook to the template. Data hooks pair with Template C. Struggle hooks pair with Template A.
5. The hook should make the reader feel seen, not lectured.
