# Class Brand Voice

This skill is the source of truth for how Class Technologies communicates. Every piece of Class marketing content starts here.

Class Technologies builds virtual classroom software that sits inside Zoom and Microsoft Teams. The product adds a learning layer to meeting tools — engagement scoring, breakout room management, LMS integration, analytics, course templates, and interactive playback. The core positioning: meeting tools were built for meetings, not learning. Class was built for learning.

## Voice Identity

Class sounds like a senior L&D consultant who's been in the field for 15 years and now builds the tools they always wished they had. Knowledgeable without being academic. Practical without being simplistic. Confident without being pushy. The voice earns trust by demonstrating understanding of real problems before offering solutions.

### Voice Constants (These Never Change)

**Credible** — Every major claim has a research citation, customer quote, or data point behind it. Class doesn't assert. Class demonstrates.

**Accessible** — Complex ideas get explained in plain terms. An L&D director and a first-year trainer should both understand the same blog post. Use contractions. Write like a person.

**Practical** — Every section of content includes something the reader can act on. Theoretical frameworks get paired with implementation steps.

**Honest** — Class acknowledges real limitations of virtual training. It doesn't pretend challenges don't exist. It shows how to address them.

**Consultant-Like** — Class positions as a thinking partner who happens to sell software, not a vendor who happens to have thoughts. Best practices come first. Product comes second.

**Outcome-Focused** — Features only matter in terms of what they enable. "Enhanced breakout rooms" is meaningless alone. "Monitor all breakout groups simultaneously so you can intervene when a group gets stuck" connects feature to outcome.

**Evidence-Driven** — Third-party research (LinkedIn Workplace Learning Report, Training Magazine surveys, academic frameworks) carries more weight than proprietary claims. Customer quotes include full name, title, and company.

**Measured** — Pragmatic optimism. Problems are solvable, but not overnight. Class accelerates what already works; it doesn't replace the work of good instructional design.

### Tone Flexes (These Adapt by Context)

Tone is the dial on top of voice. Voice stays constant. Tone shifts by audience, channel, and purpose.

| Context | Formality | Energy | Technical Depth | Emphasis |
|---------|-----------|--------|-----------------|----------|
| Blog post (corporate/gov) | 60% conversational, 40% authoritative | Measured, pragmatic | Medium — cite frameworks, explain acronyms | Problem-first, then solution |
| Website product page | Confident, benefit-led | Higher energy, concise | Low — outcomes over specs | Feature → benefit → outcome chain |
| Customer story | Warm, narrative | Let the customer carry the energy | Low-medium | Quotes do the heavy lifting |
| Social media (LinkedIn) | Professional but human | Punchy, thought-provoking | Low — one insight per post | Lead with the counterintuitive thing |
| Email campaign | Personal, helpful | Action-oriented | Low | One CTA, clear value |
| Sales collateral | Confident, specific | Benefit-driven | Medium-high | Metrics, case studies, proof |
| Government content | Reassuring, professional | Measured | Medium | Security, compliance, scale |
| Healthcare content | Serious, safety-conscious | Measured | Medium-high | Compliance, consistency, verification |
| Press release | Formal, factual | Newsworthy | Low-medium | Who, what, when, where, why |

### Writing for Corporate vs. Government

**Corporate voice adjustments:**
- Lead with talent development, retention, and business impact
- Use language: "upskill," "reskill," "onboarding," "career development," "culture of learning"
- Pain points: scattered training, tool-switching friction, lack of engagement data, inconsistent delivery
- Proof points: Volvo, BCG, Marriott, GoDaddy references

**Government voice adjustments:**
- Lead with security, compliance, and scale
- Use language: "FedRAMP-certified," "agency-wide," "compliance verification," "workforce development"
- Pain points: legacy systems, remote work adaptation, recruitment/retention, cybersecurity concerns
- Always mention: security posture, data protection, role-based access, scalable deployment

## Messaging Architecture

### Core Positioning Statement

Class adds a learning-centric layer to Zoom and Microsoft Teams, giving instructors the tools to create engaging, measurable, and repeatable virtual training at scale.

**The one-line differentiator:** Meeting tools were built for meetings. Class was built for learning.

### Four Solution Pillars

Every piece of content should connect to one or more of these pillars:

**1. Foundation** — Everyone learns, anywhere. Secure, seamless access with built-in reliability, scalability, and accessibility. Built on the platforms organizations already use.

**2. Before Training** — Seamless setup, maximum impact. Sync your LMS, build templates and assessments, pre-configure breakout rooms. Create training that's repeatable and consistent.

**3. During Training** — Stay in command. Manage breakout rooms, monitor progress, guide learners in real time. Interactive features that drive focus, participation, and outcomes.

**4. After Training** — Keep learning active, insights clear. On-demand content, interactive recordings, powerful analytics on attendance, engagement, and progress.

### Three Advantage Pillars

**Drive Learner Engagement** — Active participation, collaboration, interaction. Learners are seen, heard, and involved.

**Scale Access to Quality Instruction** — Repeatable, well-crafted virtual learning. Anyone, anywhere, same quality.

**Foster Community & Connection** — Distance doesn't limit learning together. Instructors and learners build real relationships.

## Terminology

Read [references/terminology.md](references/terminology.md) for the complete terminology bible. Key rules:

**Always use:** "Virtual Instructor-Led Training (VILT)" on first reference, "VILT" thereafter. "Purpose-built" when differentiating from meeting tools. "Engagement" to mean measurable participation, not just attendance. "Built on Zoom and Teams" (not "integrates with"). "Class sits inside Zoom and Teams" for Russell-voice contexts.

**Never use:** "Revolutionary," "cutting-edge," "game-changing," "next-level," "best-in-class," "synergy," "leverage" (as a verb), "empower" (sparingly, only in headlines), "holistic," "robust" (use "solid" or "thorough"), "ecosystem" (name the actual pieces), "innovative" (show the innovation, don't label it).

**Product references:** "Class" (not "Class Technologies" except in formal contexts). Features referenced by outcome, not name: "monitor all breakout groups from one view" (not "Bird's Eye View feature").

## How Class References Itself

This is critical. Class positions product mentions inside a best-practices framework. Pattern:

1. Introduce a real problem the audience faces
2. Explain universal best practices that address it (platform-agnostic)
3. Then show how Class enables those practices specifically
4. Support with customer quote or data point

Class is an accelerator of what's known to work. Not a magic solution. Not a replacement for good instructional design. The content should be valuable even if the reader never buys Class.

## Anti-Patterns

Read [references/anti-patterns.md](references/anti-patterns.md) for detailed examples. Core rules:

- No feature-dumping without outcome connections
- No unsourced statistics or vague metrics
- No aggressive sales language in educational content
- No competitor bashing (position against "traditional meeting tools" as a category)
- No one-size-fits-all framing
- No dismissing existing methods (respect what organizations already do)
- No artificial urgency or fear-based messaging
- No passive voice (find the actor, put them first)
- No AI writing patterns (see class-content-qa skill for detection rules)
