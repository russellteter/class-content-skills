# Anti-Patterns: What Class Content Never Does

These patterns undermine the Class brand voice. Each includes the problem, why it fails, and how to fix it.

## 1. Feature-Dumping Without Outcomes

**Bad:**
> Class offers enhanced breakout rooms, engagement scoring, LMS integration, course templates, interactive playback, auto transcription, data dashboards, seating charts, and ID verification.

**Why it fails:** A list of features tells the reader nothing about why they should care. It reads like a spec sheet, not a value proposition.

**Good:**
> When an instructor launches a breakout activity, they can see what every group is doing from one screen. If a group goes quiet or gets stuck, the instructor drops in, helps, and moves on. After the session, engagement data flows directly to the LMS — no manual exports, no spreadsheets. The instructor built the session once using a template, and the next cohort gets the same structured experience.

**Rule:** Every feature mention must connect to what a person can DO with it and what OUTCOME it produces.

## 2. Unsourced Claims

**Bad:**
> Virtual training is dramatically more effective when delivered through Class.

**Why it fails:** "Dramatically" is unquantified. No source. No evidence. Reads like marketing fluff.

**Good:**
> In a controlled study by Tripod Education Partners, learners using Class showed a five-fold increase in engagement compared to those using standard Zoom. The study used identical one-hour sessions with participants randomly assigned across three platforms.

**Rule:** Major claims need a source: research citation, customer quote with attribution, or specific metric with context.

## 3. Aggressive Sales Language in Educational Content

**Bad:**
> Don't get left behind. Your competitors are already using purpose-built training platforms. Act now before it's too late.

**Why it fails:** Fear-based urgency undermines trust. Class content educates; it doesn't pressure.

**Good:**
> 94% of L&D professionals now use VILT as part of their training process, according to a Class/Training Magazine survey. Organizations that invest in purpose-built platforms report higher completion rates and stronger engagement metrics. The shift is happening — the question is whether your platform keeps up with what your instructors need.

**Rule:** State the trend with evidence. Let the reader draw their own conclusion about urgency.

## 4. Generic Competitor Bashing

**Bad:**
> Unlike Webex and Adobe Connect, Class actually works the way trainers need it to.

**Why it fails:** Naming competitors by name in marketing content is unprofessional and legally risky. It also positions Class as reactive, not confident.

**Good:**
> General-purpose meeting tools give you video, chat, and screen share — the basics. But training isn't a meeting. Trainers need persistent templates, breakout oversight, real-time engagement data, and LMS integration. Purpose-built training platforms close that gap.

**Rule:** Position against the CATEGORY of meeting tools, not specific products. Zoom and Teams are partners, not competitors.

## 5. Passive Voice Hiding the Actor

**Bad:**
> Engagement was improved by 40% when the new platform was implemented.

**Why it fails:** Who improved it? Who implemented it? Passive voice drains energy and avoids accountability.

**Good:**
> Volvo's training team improved engagement by 40% within three months of rolling out Class across their dealer network.

**Rule:** Name the person or team doing the action. Put them at the front of the sentence.

## 6. AI Writing Patterns

These are tell-tale signs of AI-generated content. The class-content-qa skill catches these, but avoid them during writing:

**Binary contrasts:**
> "It's not about the technology. It's about the learning."

Fix: State the point directly. "The learning matters more than the tool."

**Throat-clearing openers:**
> "In today's rapidly evolving learning landscape..."

Fix: Cut the entire opener. Start with the specific thing.

**Dramatic fragmentation:**
> "Engagement. Connection. Results. That's what Class delivers."

Fix: Complete sentences. "Class gives instructors engagement data, connection tools, and measurable results."

**False agency:**
> "The platform transforms the virtual training experience."

Fix: Name the human. "Instructors transform their virtual training by using breakout oversight, real-time engagement data, and templates."

**Lazy extremes:**
> "Every organization needs this. Everyone struggles with virtual training."

Fix: Be specific. "L&D teams running 50+ virtual sessions per month struggle with consistency and engagement tracking."

**Performative emphasis:**
> "Let that sink in." / "This matters." / "Full stop."

Fix: Delete. Trust the content.

## 7. One-Size-Fits-All Framing

**Bad:**
> Class is perfect for every organization looking to improve virtual training.

**Why it fails:** Nothing is perfect for everyone. This reads as either naive or dishonest.

**Good:**
> Class is built for organizations where live instructor-led training is a core delivery method — not a nice-to-have. If your trainers run 10+ virtual sessions per month and need engagement data, LMS integration, and consistent delivery across instructors, the platform closes gaps that meeting tools leave open.

**Rule:** Qualify the audience. Class is for organizations where VILT is mission-critical.

## 8. Dismissing Existing Methods

**Bad:**
> Traditional training approaches are failing. It's time to completely rethink how your organization delivers learning.

**Why it fails:** Dismisses what organizations have built. Insulting to experienced L&D professionals. Creates defensiveness, not curiosity.

**Good:**
> Most L&D teams have strong training content and experienced instructors. The gap isn't in what they teach — it's in what their platform lets them see and do during a live virtual session. Engagement data, breakout oversight, and persistent course structures help instructors deliver the experience they already know how to create.

**Rule:** Respect what exists. Position Class as filling a specific platform gap, not replacing everything.

## 9. Vague CTAs

**Bad:**
> Contact us to learn more about our solutions.

**Why it fails:** "Learn more" is passive. "Solutions" is generic. "Contact us" is friction-heavy.

**Good:**
> Schedule a 20-minute walkthrough to see how Class handles breakout rooms, engagement scoring, and LMS integration. [Get a Live Demo]

**Rule:** CTAs name what the reader will see or get. Use action verbs: "Schedule," "See," "Explore," "Download."

## 10. Emoji and Exclamation Overuse

**Bad:**
> We're so excited to announce our new breakout room features! 🎉🚀 These updates will transform your training!!

**Why it fails:** Class's voice is measured and professional. Emojis and exclamation marks dilute authority.

**Good:**
> Class released three updates to breakout room management this quarter: pre-configured group assignments, real-time transcription across rooms, and a consolidated instructor view. Here's what each one changes for trainers.

**Rule:** One exclamation mark per piece maximum (and only in celebratory contexts like customer wins). No emojis in blog posts or website copy. LinkedIn posts may use 1-2 emojis sparingly.
